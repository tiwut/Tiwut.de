Synology NAS: Initial Setup & Configuration Guide

Introduction to Synology NAS

Synology Inc. is a company specializing in Network Attached Storage (NAS) appliances. Synology NAS devices run a Linux-based operating system called DiskStation Manager (DSM), which provides a user-friendly web interface for managing storage, sharing files, running applications, and much more.

Synology offers a wide range of NAS models, from small home units (J series, Value series) to powerful enterprise solutions (Plus series, XS/XS+ series, SA series, UC series). While specific hardware capabilities vary, the initial setup process for DSM is largely consistent.

This guide covers the general steps for unboxing, hardware setup, DSM installation, and essential initial configuration for most Synology NAS devices.

Prerequisites

Before you begin, ensure you have the following:

  • Synology NAS Device: The NAS unit itself.
  • Compatible Hard Drives (HDDs) or Solid State Drives (SSDs):
    • Check the Synology Products Compatibility List for your specific NAS model to ensure drive compatibility.
    • You'll need at least one drive to start, but most NAS setups use two or more drives for redundancy (RAID).
    • If using drives that previously contained data, this data will be erased during the DSM setup and storage pool creation. Back up any important data from these drives.
  • Network Connection:
    • An active internet connection for downloading DSM (if needed) and updates.
    • An Ethernet cable to connect the NAS to your router or network switch.
  • Computer on the Same Network: A PC or Mac connected to the same network as the NAS, used for initial setup and accessing the DSM web interface.
  • Screwdriver (Optional): Some NAS models require a Phillips screwdriver for drive installation, though many offer tool-less drive trays.
  • Time: Allow at least an hour for the initial setup, DSM installation, and storage pool creation (which can take many hours or even days depending on drive size and RAID type).

1. Unboxing & Hardware Setup

  1. Unbox Your Synology NAS: Carefully unpack the NAS unit and all included accessories (power adapter, Ethernet cable(s), drive tray keys if applicable, screws if needed, quick start guide).
  2. Familiarize Yourself with the Unit: Identify the drive bays, network ports (LAN ports), USB ports, power connector, and power button. Consult your specific model's Quick Installation Guide or Hardware Installation Guide (available on Synology's website).
  3. (If applicable) Install RAM or M.2 NVMe SSD Cache: If you purchased additional RAM or M.2 NVMe SSDs for caching (supported on certain models), install them now according to your NAS model's hardware guide. This is often easier before installing hard drives.

2. Hard Drive Installation

Ensure the NAS is powered OFF and unplugged before installing or removing drives.

  1. Open Drive Bays:
    • For models with hot-swappable trays: Unlock and pull out the drive trays.
    • For other models: You may need to remove a cover or panel as per your model's guide.
  2. Mount Drives onto Trays (or directly into bays):
    • 3.5-inch HDDs: Most Synology units have tool-less trays for 3.5-inch drives where you secure the drive with side clips or panels. Some may require screws.
    • 2.5-inch HDDs/SSDs: You'll typically need to use screws to secure 2.5-inch drives to the trays. Adapter brackets might be needed or included for some models.
    Ensure the drive's connectors (SATA power and data) are facing the correct way to align with the backplane in the NAS.
  3. Insert Drive Trays: Slide the drive trays (with drives installed) back into the NAS until they click securely into place. Ensure they are fully seated. Lock them if your model has tray locks.
  4. Number of Drives:
    • You can start with one drive, but for data redundancy using RAID (e.g., SHR, RAID 1, RAID 5, RAID 6), you'll need two or more drives.
    • It's generally recommended to use drives of the same model and capacity for optimal RAID performance and compatibility, though Synology Hybrid RAID (SHR) offers more flexibility with mixed drive sizes.

3. Network & Power Connection

  1. Connect Ethernet Cable: Connect one end of an Ethernet cable to one of the LAN ports on your Synology NAS and the other end to an available port on your router or network switch.

    If your NAS has multiple LAN ports, connect to LAN 1 initially unless you have specific network bonding plans.

  2. Connect Power Adapter: Connect the AC power adapter to the NAS and plug the other end into a power outlet.

    It's highly recommended to connect your NAS to an Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) to protect against data corruption from power outages.

  3. Power On the NAS: Press the power button on the front or back of the NAS. Wait for it to boot up. This may take a few minutes. You should see status LEDs light up.

4. Finding Your NAS on the Network

Once the NAS has booted (usually indicated by a beep and stable status LEDs), you need to find its IP address to access the setup wizard.

Methods to find your NAS:

  1. Synology Web Assistant:
    • On a computer connected to the same network, open a web browser and go to http://find.synology.com or http://diskstation:5000 (for DiskStation models) or http://rackstation:5000 (for RackStation models).
    • Web Assistant should scan your local network and list your Synology NAS. Click on it to connect.
  2. Synology Assistant (Desktop Utility):
    • Download and install the Synology Assistant utility from the Synology Download Center for your computer's OS.
    • Launch Synology Assistant. It will scan your network for Synology devices.
    • Select your NAS from the list and click "Connect".
  3. Router's DHCP Client List:
    • Log in to your router's administration interface.
    • Look for a "DHCP Client List," "Connected Devices," or similar section. Find your Synology NAS (it might be listed by its model name or MAC address) and note its assigned IP address.
    • Enter this IP address into your web browser (e.g., http://192.168.1.105). Add :5000 if it doesn't connect directly (e.g. http://192.168.1.105:5000).

5. DiskStation Manager (DSM) Installation & Setup

Once you connect to your NAS via a web browser using one of the methods above, the DSM setup wizard will begin.

  1. Welcome & EULA: You'll see a welcome screen. Click "Set up". Accept the End User License Agreement (EULA).
  2. Install DSM:
    • Automatic Download & Install (Recommended): If your NAS has internet access, it will offer to download and install the latest version of DSM automatically. This is the easiest method. Click "Install Now".
    • Manual Install: If you downloaded a .pat DSM installation file from Synology's website, you can choose "Manual Install" and browse to the file. This is useful for offline installations or specific version requirements.

    This step will format the hard drives you installed in the NAS. Confirm that you want to proceed, understanding all data on those drives will be erased.

    The NAS will now download (if applicable) and install DSM. This process will take around 10-15 minutes, and the NAS will reboot automatically.
  3. Create Administrator Account: After reboot, the wizard will continue.
    • Server Name: Give your NAS a unique name (e.g., MySynologyNAS).
    • Username: Create an administrator username. Avoid using common names like admin or root for better security.
    • Password: Create a strong password for this administrator account. Confirm it.
    Click "Next".
  4. DSM Update and Maintenance:
    • Choose how you want DSM updates to be handled:
      • "Install important DSM updates automatically (recommended)"
      • "Install important DSM updates and all package updates automatically"
      • "Notify me and let me install updates manually"
    • You can also configure a schedule for S.M.A.R.T. tests for your drives. Defaults are usually fine to start.
    Click "Next".
  5. Synology Account (Optional but Recommended):
    • You'll be prompted to sign in with or create a Synology Account.
    • A Synology Account enables features like QuickConnect (easy remote access), technical support, extended warranty registration, and notifications.
    • You can "Sign In," "Create an account," or "Skip this step" (you can set it up later).
  6. QuickConnect ID (If setting up Synology Account): If you opt-in for QuickConnect, create a unique QuickConnect ID. This allows you to access your NAS remotely without complex port forwarding (e.g., http://QuickConnect.to/YourID).
  7. Device Analytics (Optional): Choose whether to share anonymous device analytics with Synology to help improve products. Click "Submit" or "Skip".
  8. You Are All Set: You'll see a confirmation page. Click "Go" to be taken to the DSM desktop.

6. Create Your First Storage Pool and Volume

After the initial DSM setup, you need to create a storage pool and at least one volume to start storing data.

Storage Manager will likely open automatically for new setups. If not, open it from the DSM main menu.

  1. In Storage Manager, you'll be guided to create a storage pool.
    • Choose a RAID Type:
      • SHR (Synology Hybrid RAID): Recommended for most home users and beginners. It simplifies storage expansion and allows mixing drive sizes (with some caveats). SHR-1 offers 1-drive fault tolerance; SHR-2 offers 2-drive fault tolerance (requires more drives).
      • Basic: Single drive, no redundancy. Not recommended unless you have a robust backup plan.
      • RAID 0 (Stripe): Combines drives for performance, no redundancy. Not recommended for critical data.
      • RAID 1 (Mirror): Duplicates data across two drives. 1-drive fault tolerance.
      • RAID 5: Needs at least 3 drives. 1-drive fault tolerance. Good balance of capacity and redundancy.
      • RAID 6: Needs at least 4 drives. 2-drive fault tolerance. Higher redundancy than RAID 5.
      • RAID 10 (Stripe of Mirrors): Needs at least 4 drives (even numbers). Good performance and redundancy.
      Select your preferred RAID type based on the number of drives and your needs.
    • Select Drives: Choose the drives you want to include in this storage pool.
    • Drive Check (Optional but Recommended): The wizard may ask if you want to perform a drive check. This can take a long time but helps identify faulty drives early.
    • Confirm Settings: Review the summary and confirm.

      The selected drives will be formatted and all data on them erased.

  2. Create Volume: Once the storage pool is created, you'll be prompted to create a volume on it.
    • Filesystem: Choose Btrfs (recommended for features like data checksumming, snapshots, and shared folder quotas) or ext4. Btrfs is the default and preferred choice for most modern Synology features.
    • Allocate Size: You can allocate the entire pool capacity to one volume or create multiple volumes with specific sizes. For simplicity, using the maximum available space for one volume is common.
  3. Storage Manager will now create and optimize/synchronize the storage pool and volume. This process (especially RAID synchronization or parity checks) can take many hours or even days depending on drive sizes and RAID type. You can still use DSM during this time, but performance might be impacted. Monitor progress in Storage Manager.

7. Create Shared Folders

Shared folders are the top-level directories where you will store your data and grant access to users.

  1. Open Control Panel from the DSM main menu.
  2. Go to Shared Folder.
  3. Click Create > Create Shared Folder.
  4. Basic Information:
    • Name: Enter a name for the shared folder (e.g., Documents, Photos, Backups).
    • Description (Optional): Add a description.
    • Location: Select the volume where this shared folder will reside.
    • Options:
      • Hide this shared folder in "My Network Places".
      • Hide sub-folders and files from users without permissions.
      • Enable Recycle Bin: Highly recommended. You can restrict access to administrators.
    Click "Next".
  5. Encryption (Optional): You can choose to encrypt the shared folder for added security. If you do, you'll need to manage an encryption key. Click "Next".
  6. Advanced Data Integrity (Btrfs only, Optional): If using Btrfs, you can enable "Enable data checksum for advanced data integrity". Recommended. Click "Next".
  7. Configure User Permissions: Assign read/write (RW), read-only (RO), or no access (NA) permissions for different users or groups to this shared folder. You'll create users in the next step; you can come back to edit permissions later. For now, your administrator account will have access. Click "Apply" or "OK".

Repeat for any other top-level shared folders you need.

8. Create User Accounts

It's best practice to create non-administrator user accounts for daily access to files.

  1. Open Control Panel.
  2. Go to User & Group (or just "User" on older DSM).
  3. On the "User" tab, click Create.
  4. User Information:
    • Name (Username): The login name.
    • Description (Optional).
    • Email (Optional): For password reset notifications.
    • Password: Set a strong password. Confirm it.
    • Options like "Disallow the user to change account password" or "Password is always valid".
    Click "Next".
  5. Join Groups: Add the user to relevant groups (e.g., users is default). You can create custom groups later. Click "Next".
  6. Assign Shared Folder Permissions: For each shared folder, set permissions (No access, Read-only, Read/Write) for this new user. Click "Next".
  7. Assign Quota (Optional): Set storage quotas for the user on specific volumes. Click "Next".
  8. Assign Application Permissions: Control access to installed applications (e.g., File Station, Synology Photos). Click "Next".
  9. User Speed Limit (Optional): Set upload/download speed limits for various services. Click "Next".
  10. Confirm Settings: Review and click "Apply" or "Done".

Create accounts for all individuals who will access the NAS.

9. Install Essential Packages from Package Center

Package Center is Synology's app store. Install packages to extend your NAS's functionality.

  1. Open Package Center from the DSM main menu.
  2. Browse or search for packages. Some essentials or highly recommended ones:
    • Synology Drive Server: For private cloud file syncing and sharing (like Dropbox/Google Drive).
    • Synology Photos: Photo management, sharing, and mobile backup.
    • Hyper Backup: Robust backup solution for backing up your NAS data to various destinations (another Synology NAS, USB drive, cloud services).
    • Snapshot Replication (Btrfs only): For creating point-in-time snapshots of shared folders and LUNs, and replicating them.
    • Antivirus Essential or Antivirus by McAfee: For on-NAS virus scanning.
    • Universal Search: For fast searching of files and metadata.
    • Consider others based on your needs: Plex Media Server, Docker, Virtual Machine Manager, Note Station, Calendar, etc.
  3. Click "Install" for desired packages. Some may have additional setup steps after installation.

10. Security & Updates

Keeping your NAS secure and up-to-date is crucial.

  1. DSM Updates:
    • Go to Control Panel > Update & Restore.
    • Check your "Update Settings" from the DSM setup. Ensure it's set to automatically download/install important updates or notify you.
    • Regularly check for and apply DSM updates.
  2. Package Updates:
    • Open Package Center. Go to the "Installed" tab.
    • Update any packages that show an "Update" button. You can enable auto-updates for packages in Package Center settings.
  3. Security Advisor:
    • Open Security Advisor from the main menu.
    • Run a scan. It will check for security vulnerabilities and offer recommendations (e.g., strong passwords, firewall settings, disabling unused services). Address any issues found.
  4. Firewall:
    • Go to Control Panel > Security > Firewall.
    • Enable the firewall and create rules to allow only necessary services and IP addresses/regions to access your NAS. Start with default rules and refine.
  5. Account Protection & Auto Block: (Control Panel > Security > Account / Auto Block) Enable these features to protect against brute-force login attacks.
  6. Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): (DSM User Icon > Personal > Account / Security) Enable 2FA for your administrator and user accounts for significantly improved security.
  7. Disable Unused Services: (Control Panel > File Services / Application Privileges) Disable any services or applications you don't use to reduce the attack surface.
